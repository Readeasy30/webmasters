# New Puppy Checklist

Bringing home a puppy is exciting.

It can also feel like a lot.

This checklist helps you get ready with simple supplies, safe routines, and clear next steps.

> **Pet safety note:** This page gives general pet-care information. It does not replace a veterinarian. Call your vet if symptoms are serious, sudden, painful, or getting worse.

## Quick Start Checklist

Before your puppy comes home, prepare:

- food and water bowls
- puppy food
- collar or harness
- leash
- ID tag
- crate or safe sleeping area
- puppy pads or potty supplies
- chew toys
- cleaning supplies
- brush or grooming tool
- vet appointment
- safe place away from hazards

## First Vet Visit

Schedule a vet visit soon after bringing your puppy home.

Ask about:

- vaccines
- deworming
- flea and tick prevention
- heartworm prevention
- food amount
- growth and weight
- spay/neuter timing
- microchip options

Bring any paperwork you received from the breeder, shelter, rescue, or previous owner.

## Puppy-Proof Your Home

Puppies chew, sniff, and explore.

Before the puppy arrives, check for:

- loose cords
- small objects
- medicine
- cleaning products
- trash cans
- houseplants
- open doors or gaps
- sharp objects
- foods that can be unsafe for dogs

Keep unsafe items out of reach.

## Food And Water

Use a puppy food that fits your puppy's age and size.

Do not change food suddenly unless your vet recommends it. A slow food change can help avoid stomach upset.

Keep fresh water available.

## Sleep Area

Give your puppy a quiet place to rest.

A crate, puppy pen, or small safe room can help your puppy feel secure.

Keep the area clean and calm.

## Potty Routine

Puppies need frequent potty breaks.

Take your puppy out:

- after waking up
- after eating
- after play
- before bedtime
- any time they sniff, circle, or seem restless

Praise calm success. Do not scare or punish your puppy for accidents.

## Chewing And Toys

Chewing is normal for puppies.

Give safe chew toys and remove items you do not want chewed.

Avoid toys that are too small, broken, sharp, or easy to swallow.

## Family Rules

Before the puppy comes home, decide:

- where the puppy sleeps
- who feeds the puppy
- who handles potty breaks
- what rooms are off limits
- how children should interact with the puppy
- what words the family will use for training

Simple rules prevent confusion.

## Children And Puppies

Teach children to:

- use gentle hands
- avoid pulling ears or tails
- let the puppy sleep
- not bother the puppy while eating
- ask an adult before picking up the puppy

Adult supervision is important.

## Warning Signs To Watch For

Call your veterinarian if your puppy has:

- repeated vomiting
- repeated diarrhea
- trouble breathing
- refusal to eat
- extreme tiredness
- coughing that gets worse
- pale gums
- swelling
- signs of pain
- possible toxin exposure

## Common Questions

### How soon should a puppy see a vet?

Soon after coming home. Your vet can check health, vaccines, parasite prevention, food, and growth.

### Should I let my puppy sleep in my bed?

That is a family choice, but many puppies do better with a safe, consistent sleeping area at first.

### What if my puppy will not eat?

A puppy that refuses food may need vet advice, especially if they also seem tired, sick, or weak.

### What is the most important first step?

Make the home safe and schedule a vet visit.

## Final Reminder

A puppy needs safety, routine, patience, and vet care.

Start simple. Keep the home safe. Ask your veterinarian when you are unsure.
